# Six Thinking Hats

@note
  Six Thinking Hats is a structured thinking method introduced by Edward
  de Bono. It separates thinking into six distinct **modes**, each
  represented by a coloured hat, so a person or group can deliberately
  examine a subject from one perspective at a time without the modes
  contaminating each other.

  - **White Hat** — Facts and information. What do we know? What do we not
    know? What data is missing? Stay neutral; report, do not interpret.
  - **Red Hat** — Emotions, feelings, intuition, hunches. State them
    without justification. The goal is to surface the affective layer that
    otherwise gets hidden behind rationalisation.
  - **Black Hat** — Caution, judgment, risks. Where could this fail? What
    are the weaknesses, downsides, or integration problems? Critical
    thinking under discipline, not pessimism for its own sake.
  - **Yellow Hat** — Optimism, value, benefits. Where is the upside? What
    has to be true for this to succeed? Justified positivity, not cheering.
  - **Green Hat** — Creativity, alternatives, new ideas. What else could
    we do? What provocations or lateral moves open new options? This is the
    generative hat.
  - **Blue Hat** — Process and overview. What is the question we are
    answering? Which hats should we wear in what order? What is the
    summary, conclusion, and decision?

  The discipline comes from **wearing one hat at a time** and from a
  facilitator (the Blue Hat) who manages the sequence. This avoids the
  common failure where one person argues from emotion while another
  argues from facts, and they never engage on the same plane.

  Six Hats is best applied to: meetings that need to reach a decision,
  proposals that need balanced evaluation, ideas that need both creative
  expansion and critical pressure, and any situation where one mode of
  thinking is dominating and crowding out the others.

  This is a **pure method definition**. It assumes a subject, objective, and
  constraints when they are available; otherwise it provides a generic Six Hats
  framing.

  Reference:
  - Edward de Bono (1985). "Six Thinking Hats."

You are applying the Six Thinking Hats method.

Your objective is to analyse the subject defined by the source context
by deliberately wearing each of the six hats — **White, Red, Black, Yellow,
Green, Blue** — one at a time. Each hat must be used in its proper mode and
must not bleed into another mode.

Use Six Hats as a disciplined thinking method, not as decorative formatting.
Each hat's section must contain content that could only have come from that
mode of thinking. If a section reads as if any other hat could have written
it, the discipline has failed.

## Core Six-Hats Principles

- **One hat at a time.** Inside a hat's section, stay strictly in that mode.
  Do not slip into evaluation while wearing the White Hat, and do not
  rationalise while wearing the Red Hat.
- **Parallel thinking, not adversarial.** All hats look in the same
  direction together. The Black Hat is not the opposition to the Yellow
  Hat; they are two passes over the same subject by the same thinker.
- **The Blue Hat owns the process.** It defines the question, sets the
  sequence, manages time, and produces the final summary. It is the only
  hat allowed to talk *about* the thinking rather than do it.
- **Discipline beats inspiration.** The value of the method is that it
  forces modes that would otherwise be skipped (often Red, often Green,
  often a rigorous Black). Skipping or under-using a hat defeats the
  method.
- **Distinguish observation from interpretation.** The White Hat reports
  what is known and what is missing. It does not draw conclusions, propose
  causes, or recommend actions.
- **Distinguish feeling from justification.** The Red Hat states the
  feeling, briefly. It does not defend it. If the section starts
  explaining *why* the feeling is right, the hat has slipped.
- **Distinguish critique from negativity.** The Black Hat names risks,
  weaknesses, and failure modes with reasons. "I just do not like it" is
  Red, not Black.
- **Distinguish benefits from cheerleading.** The Yellow Hat names upside
  with the conditions required for it to materialise. Unconditional
  enthusiasm is a sign the hat has slipped.
- **Distinguish ideation from evaluation.** The Green Hat generates and
  provokes. Killing ideas inside the Green Hat section is a Black Hat
  intrusion.

## Required Six-Hats Workflow

1. **Blue Hat — Frame.** State the precise question the session is meant
   to answer, the scope, the time horizon, the decision at stake, and the
   sequence of hats to be worn. (Default sequence below is a reasonable
   starting point.)
2. **White Hat — Information.** List what is known, what is uncertain,
   and what is missing. Separate facts from inferences. Note the source
   or confidence of each item where it matters.
3. **Red Hat — Feelings.** State the emotional reactions, intuitions, and
   gut feelings the subject provokes — both your own and those that key
   stakeholders are likely to have. Brief, declarative, no justification.
4. **Yellow Hat — Benefits.** Identify the value, opportunities, and
   upside, with the conditions under which each benefit holds.
5. **Black Hat — Risks.** Identify the weaknesses, dangers, failure
   modes, and reasons the subject could not work, each with the
   mechanism that produces the harm.
6. **Green Hat — Alternatives.** Generate new ideas, modifications,
   provocations, and lateral moves. Include at least one option that
   reframes the question itself. Do not evaluate inside this section.
7. **Blue Hat — Summary and Decision.** Stand back. Reconcile the
   outputs of the other hats, note where they reinforce or contradict
   each other, and produce a clear conclusion, decision, or set of next
   steps. Flag any hat whose output was thin and explain why.

The default sequence above (Blue → White → Red → Yellow → Black → Green
→ Blue) is suitable for evaluating a proposal. For generating new options,
prefer Blue → White → Green → Yellow → Black → Red → Blue. State the
chosen sequence explicitly in the opening Blue Hat section.

## Hat-by-Hat Prompting Questions

Use these as starting points; extend them to fit the subject.

- **Blue** — What exactly are we trying to decide or produce? What is in
  scope and out of scope? Which hats should we use, in what order, and
  for how long? What outcome marks this session as complete?
- **White** — What facts do we have? From where? How confident are we?
  What data is missing? What would we need to know to decide well? What
  is a known unknown vs an assumed unknown?
- **Red** — What is the gut reaction? What feelings are present —
  enthusiasm, unease, fear, suspicion, attraction, fatigue? Whose
  feelings are these, and whose are we not capturing?
- **Yellow** — What is the best plausible outcome? Who benefits, and
  how much? Under what conditions does the upside materialise? What
  precedents support optimism? What hidden value might be unlocked?
- **Black** — What could go wrong? What weaknesses, risks, or
  conflicts exist? Where does the logic break under stress?
  Which assumptions are fragile? What second-order effects could harm
  someone?
- **Green** — What alternatives have not been considered? What
  modifications would change the picture? What provocations or lateral
  moves open new options? What would the opposite of this look like —
  and what does that reveal?
- **Blue (closing)** — What is the conclusion across all hats? Where do
  hats agree and where do they pull in different directions? What is the
  decision, the recommendation, or the next concrete step? What thinking
  is still missing?

## Common Failure Modes

- Letting the Black Hat dominate. A loud Black Hat with a quiet Yellow
  and Green Hat is the most common failure of the method.
- Treating Red as something to skip "because we are being rational."
  Unstated emotions still drive decisions; the Red Hat surfaces them so
  the rest of the analysis can be honest.
- Writing the White Hat as a mixture of facts and conclusions. If a
  sentence has the word "therefore" in it, it belongs in another hat.
- Using the Green Hat to refine existing ideas rather than to generate
  new ones. Refinement is fine, but it cannot be the whole section.
- A weak Blue Hat that does not frame the question or produce a real
  conclusion. The session then ends without a decision, and the value
  of the method is lost.
- Treating the hats as personality types ("she is a Black Hat person").
  The whole point is that one thinker uses every hat in turn.
- Wearing two hats at once. Phrases like "I feel this is risky because…"
  are Red bleeding into Black. Separate them.

## Presentation Rules

- If the source task already specifies a deliverable format, honor it,
  but still make all six hats visible and clearly separated, in the order
  chosen by the opening Blue Hat.
- Label each hat section clearly with the hat name and its mode.
- Keep each hat's content in that mode. If you must reference another
  hat's content, do so explicitly ("the Black Hat noted X; here in
  Yellow, the corresponding upside is Y").
- The closing Blue Hat must produce a clear summary, conclusion, or
  decision — not a restatement of the other hats.
- If a hat's output is thin, say so in the closing Blue Hat and explain
  why (e.g., insufficient data for White, no obvious provocations for
  Green).

## Default Response Structure

### Blue Hat — Frame

State the question, the scope and boundary, the time horizon, the
decision at stake, and the sequence of hats to be worn in this session.

### White Hat — Information

What is known, what is uncertain, what is missing. Facts only; no
interpretation. Note confidence and sources where they matter.

### Red Hat — Feelings

Emotional reactions, intuitions, and gut feelings — your own and those
likely to be present in key stakeholders. Brief and declarative.

### Yellow Hat — Benefits

Plausible upside, with the conditions under which each benefit holds.
Optimism with reasons, not cheerleading.

### Black Hat — Risks

Weaknesses, dangers, and failure modes, each with the mechanism that
produces the harm. Critical thinking with reasons, not pessimism for
its own sake.

### Green Hat — Alternatives

New ideas, modifications, and provocations. Include at least one option
that reframes the question itself. Do not evaluate here.

### Blue Hat — Summary and Decision

Reconcile the outputs of all hats. Where do they agree, where do they
pull apart? State the conclusion, the recommendation, or the next
concrete step. Flag any hat whose output was thin and explain why.
