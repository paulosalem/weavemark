# Analysis of Competing Hypotheses Core

Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH) is a structured analytic technique for
comparing multiple plausible explanations against the same body of evidence.
It gives special weight to evidence that disconfirms a hypothesis. The goal is
not to prove a favored explanation, but to identify the hypothesis that is
least inconsistent with the available evidence.

## Core ACH obligations

- Evaluate competing hypotheses in parallel rather than one by one.
- Prioritize disconfirming evidence over confirming evidence.
- Weight diagnostic value more heavily than raw evidence count.
- Separate observed facts, assumptions, interpretations, indicators, missing
  evidence, and unknowns.
- Treat missing evidence carefully: absence matters only when the evidence
  should reasonably exist and be observable.
- Prefer hypotheses that are mutually distinguishable and decision-relevant. If
  reality is multi-causal, frame hypotheses as competing dominant mechanisms.
- Revise the hypothesis set if it is incomplete, overlapping, or poorly framed.
- Keep concrete examples, cases, incidents, observations, or passages visible
  next to the principle, hypothesis, diagnostic row, or judgment they support.
- State uncertainty explicitly and identify what evidence could change the
  ranking.

## Required ACH workflow

1. Define the analytic question in one sentence.
2. Construct a set of mutually distinguishable hypotheses. Aim for 3-7 when the
   situation supports it.
3. Break the available information into discrete evidence items or indicators.
4. For each evidence item, assess its relationship to each hypothesis using:
   - C = consistent
   - I = inconsistent
   - N = neutral or non-diagnostic
   - M = missing but expected
5. Mark reliability and diagnostic value for each evidence item as high,
   medium, or low.
6. Highlight the evidence that most strongly disconfirms each hypothesis.
7. Eliminate or downgrade hypotheses materially inconsistent with the strongest
   evidence.
8. Rank remaining hypotheses from least inconsistent to most inconsistent.
9. State uncertainty, assumptions, possible biases, and data-quality limits.
10. Identify the next evidence questions or collection actions that would most
    reduce uncertainty or flip the ranking.

## Guardrails

- Do not choose a hypothesis because it is intuitive, familiar, or convenient.
- Do not treat assumptions, rumors, or unverified reports as confirmed facts.
- Do not rely on evidence volume alone; one highly diagnostic inconsistency can
  outweigh many weak supporting points.
- Do not hide deception, selection bias, measurement error, or reporting lag.
- Do not force certainty when evidence is sparse, ambiguous, or conflicting.
- If none of the listed hypotheses fits well, say so and introduce a better
  alternative.
