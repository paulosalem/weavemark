# Indirect Strategy Principles

@note
  A reusable strategy lens for lawful, ethical competitive and crisis
  analysis. It is adapted from the strategic theory of Sir Basil Henry
  Liddell Hart, especially his book Strategy (2nd rev. ed., 1967),
  commonly published or subtitled as Strategy: The Indirect Approach.

  Credit/reference:
    B. H. Liddell Hart, Strategy, 2nd revised edition. London:
    Faber & Faber, 1967. Also published as Strategy: The Indirect
    Approach.

Indirect strategy seeks objectives through leverage, timing, optionality,
surprise, and pressure on weak points rather than through direct confrontation
where resistance is strongest. It is useful when resources are constrained,
counterparties can adapt, timing matters, or obvious moves are already defended.

## Core doctrine

Prefer routes that achieve the objective by changing the situation before the
main commitment is made. Avoid direct contests against prepared strength when a
different sequence, target, timing, coalition, framing, or option set can make
the objective easier, safer, or more durable.

## Maxims of Strategy

1. **Adjust ends to means.** Keep your objectives within the realistic
   capacity of your resources. Overambition is the most common cause
   of strategic failure.

2. **Keep your objective always in mind.** Every action should serve
   the ultimate goal. Do not be diverted by opportunities that do not
   advance it, and do not confuse activity with progress.

3. **Choose the line of least expectation.** Move where your opponent
   does not anticipate. The best strategy is one that the adversary
   only recognizes after it is too late to counter.

4. **Exploit the line of least resistance** — so long as it leads
   toward your objective. The path that meets the least opposition
   conserves your strength for the decisive moment.

5. **Take a line of operation that offers alternative objectives.**
   A plan that threatens multiple targets simultaneously puts the
   opponent in a dilemma — they cannot defend everywhere. If one
   path is blocked, you can pivot to another without losing momentum.

6. **Ensure that both plan and dispositions are flexible.** Rigidity
   is fatal. The ability to adapt faster than the opponent is itself
   a decisive advantage.

7. **Do not commit heavily while resistance is fully prepared.** Wait for or
   create a moment of dislocation — when the system is off-balance,
   overextended, distracted, or constrained. Striking prepared resistance wastes
   strength.

8. **Do not renew an attack along the same line after it has failed.**
   If a course of action has been tried and blocked, repeating it
   with more force rarely changes the outcome. Find a new angle.

## The Principle of Dislocation

Before attempting decisive action, first **dislocate** the current equilibrium:
change incentives, timing, information, stakeholder alignment, resource flows,
or perceived options. A dislocated system can often be changed with less cost
and less risk than a balanced, prepared one.

## Application Guidance

When analyzing any competitive or adversarial situation, stay within lawful,
ethical, nonviolent, and safety-preserving options:

- **Identify where the opponent is strongest** — and avoid that.
- **Identify where they are weakest, most rigid, or most complacent**
  — and concentrate there.
- **Prefer strategies with multiple payoffs** over single-point bets.
- **Value speed and surprise** over raw force.
- **Preserve flexibility** — the side that runs out of options first
  usually loses.
- **Consider the psychological dimension** — morale, confidence, and
  the will to act matter as much as material factors.
- **Beware the lure of the direct approach** — it feels decisive but
  is usually the most costly path and the one the opponent is most
  prepared to counter.
