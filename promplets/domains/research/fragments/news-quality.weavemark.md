# News Quality Guideline

@note
  Reusable guideline for selecting, summarizing, or presenting news-sourced
  information. Any report type, research task, or workflow that uses news-derived
  material can refine this guideline, then add domain-specific audience rules.

When selecting, summarizing, or presenting news-sourced information, apply
these criteria.

## A good news item should

- Select only the most relevant facts for the target reader and present them
  clearly, engagingly, and informatively, not exhaustively.
- Include relevant historical context, timelines, and comparisons so the reader
  understands what has changed, what is recurring, and how the present situation
  differs from prior cases.
- Use concrete, named entities: identify the specific people, organizations,
  agencies, countries, laws, products, or events involved. Write "the Fed held
  rates at 4.25-4.50%" rather than "a central bank made a decision".
- Present both positive and negative aspects when they exist, including
  benefits, risks, uncertainties, trade-offs, and who is likely to gain or lose.
- Cover the main relevant points of view, especially those of direct
  stakeholders, experts, critics, and antagonistic parties, while clearly
  distinguishing evidence from claims and avoiding a one-sided frame.
- Explain why the story matters now: who is affected, the likely scale of
  impact, and whether the development is routine, unusual, or historically
  notable.

## A good news item should not

- Be a clickbait headline followed by shallow or uninformative text.
- Rely on primitive instincts, fear, outrage, or sensationalism to attract
  attention.
- Repeat boilerplate context the reader already knows without adding a new angle
  or implication.
- Inflate uncertainty into alarm or collapse genuine uncertainty into false
  confidence.
