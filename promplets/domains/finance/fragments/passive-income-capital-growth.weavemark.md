# Financial Domain: Passive Income and Capital Growth

Passive income and capital growth form a planning frame for understanding how
income produced by assets can support consumption while preserving, and ideally
growing, the capital base that produces that income.

### Core Concepts

- Passive income source: any recurring or expected income stream produced by
  invested capital, rental assets, royalties, private credit, dividends, coupons,
  distributions, or similar sources.
- Capital base: the invested assets that should continue compounding unless the
  person explicitly models principal drawdown.
- Safe-to-spend amount: income that remains available after taxes, reserves,
  reinvestment, and capital-protection buffers.
- Growth target: the desired real or nominal growth rate for the capital base
  after consumption.
- Confidence level: how reliable a forecasted income amount is, based on source
  history, contractual certainty, volatility, concentration, and market
  sensitivity.

### Core Distinctions

- Income generated by capital is different from selling capital.
- Confirmed income is different from projected income.
- Recurring income is different from one-time inflows.
- Passive-income projections are planning aids, not financial advice, guarantees,
  or promises of returns.
- A plan that depends on reducing principal is a principal-drawdown plan unless
  the drawdown is explicitly modeled.
- Negative outcomes have specific names: shortfall, capital erosion risk, stale
  assumption, income delayed, unsafe consumption, or insufficient reinvestment.
- Capital-growth tradeoffs appear whenever consumption, taxes, reserves, or
  missed income reduce reinvestment.

### Material Information

The information that materially changes judgment, forecasting correctness, or
trust is intentionally small:

- An income source has a name, currency, expected amount, payment cadence, next
  expected payment date, confidence level, market sensitivity, and last
  confirmation date.
- A capital pool has current value, currency, growth target, reinvestment rule,
  and whether principal drawdown is allowed.
- A monthly plan has expected income, confirmed income, tax estimate, reserve
  target, planned reinvestment, safe-to-spend amount, and actual consumption.
- A forecast has an assumption set, scenario label, generated timestamp, and
  warning flags.

### Vocabulary

The natural vocabulary includes financial independence, passive income, capital
base, capital growth, safe-to-spend, reinvestment, reserve, forecast,
confidence, and capital erosion. Guarantee language is misleading: passive
income is not guaranteed income, expected returns are not guaranteed returns, and
wealth growth is not automatic.
